Geodesic Dome Mathematics

Based on "Geodesic Domes" by Tom Davis, Berkeley Math Circle, September 2004.

The Icosahedron

A geodesic dome starts with a regular icosahedron — 20 equilateral triangular faces, 12 vertices, 30 edges. At each vertex, 5 triangles meet.

The 12 vertices use the golden ratio φ = (1+√5)/2 ≈ 1.618:

(±1, ±φ, 0) cyclically permuted → (0, ±1, ±φ) → (±φ, 0, ±1)

All vertices lie on a sphere of radius √(1+φ²) ≈ 1.902. Normalized to unit sphere for subdivision.

Subdivision & Projection

Each of the 20 triangular faces is subdivided into f² smaller triangles using a barycentric lattice:

  • Each edge is divided into f equal segments
  • Smaller triangles fill the face in a regular grid
  • All new vertices are projected outward to the sphere surface (normalized)

This produces a spherical polyhedron approximating a sphere. The frequency f (2, 3, 4...) determines the dome's complexity.

Strut Counts

Full Sphere

For a Class I icosahedron sphere at frequency f:

VerticesV = 10f² + 2
Edges (struts)E = 30f²
FacesF = 20f²

Even-V Dome (Hemisphere)

E_dome = 30f²/2 + 5f/2

Examples: 2V = 65 struts, 4V = 250 struts. Flat equator — dome sits perfectly on level ground.

Odd-V Dome (Smaller)

E_dome = (30f² − 10f)/2

Examples: 3V = 120 struts. No natural equator — base is slightly non-planar.
